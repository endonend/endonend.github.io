<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386056">As part of last week's job report, the <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2012/05/05/business/economy/us-added-only-115000-jobs-in-april-rate-is-8-1.html?_r=2&amp;hp">NY Times gave us a little insight</a> into why corporations and the elite don't really mind our current economy and why things aren't going to drastically change (for the rest of us) any time soon:</p>

<blockquote id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386062">Today the United States economy is producing even more goods and services than it did when the recession officially began in December 2007, but with about five million fewer workers.</p></blockquote>

<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386066">Anyone who works knows what it's like during "uncertainty" or "bad times": folks get laid off and the rest of us have to pick up the slack. Why would businesses go back to a "bloated" workforce, when they can get pre-recession productivity out of a recession-era workforce? There's only one answer to that question: a massive increase in demand.</p>

<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386069">Where is that demand going to come from?</p>

<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386072">Europe? Nope.</p>

<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386075">Asia? Probably not.</p>

<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386078">Our government? Not now, with the massive state &amp; local cuts or the federal "austerity now!" meme. In fact, the <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2012/05/04/business/economy/off-the-charts-shrinking-government.html?ref=economy">government sector shrank during the first 3 years of the Obama administration</a> -- for the first time in 40 years. Obama has to be the worst socialist ever, right?</p>

<img src="http://endonend.micro.blog/uploads/2018/294e1fc480.jpg" title="6a00d83451c45669e20167662d473c970b-550wi" alt=""/>

<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386093">(Just imagine where the economy and unemployment rate would be, if the government had maintained a historical level of growth during this time, much less the kind of growth normally seen during a recession. The <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/economics/2012/05/08/unemployment-rate-without-government-cuts-7-1/">Wall Street Journal makes an attempt</a>: 7.1% unemployment. )</p>

<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386099">So that leaves us: your common, everyday American.</p>

<p id="yui_3_17_2_1_1407674114902_386102">But how can we do that when only <a href="http://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2011/06/30/the-wageless-profitable-recovery/">1% of the growth in national income during the recovery went to wages and salaries</a>. 88% ($464 billion, as of the end of 2010) went to corporate profits, which are at record levels. (<a href="http://www.thehackettgroup.com/about/research-alerts-press-releases/2011/12082011-companies-hoard-cash.jsp">Which they are hoarding at record levels too</a>. To the tune of $850+ billion.)</p>
